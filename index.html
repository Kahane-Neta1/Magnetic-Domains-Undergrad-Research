Magnetic Domains

Undergraduate Research Project

We investigate the field-driven relaxation dynamics of magnetic domains in a garnet film, imaged via Faraday microscopy. Applying discrete voltage steps across a coil to change the external magnetic field, we characterize three qualitatively distinct domain responses - thinning, movement, and disappearance - and track their relative contributions as a function of field step magnitude. Domain width decreases monotonically with increasing field until a critical minimum is reached, beyond which domains collapse in discrete jumps punctuated by intervals of partial equilibrium. The disappearance rate decays exponentially in time after each step, with a decay constant that is similar across different voltage jump amplitudes, indicating that the per-domain vanishing probability is intrinsic to the material rather than set by the stimulus. Geometric analysis shows that highly curved and large-area domains are preferentially eliminated, consistent with surface-energy minimization driving the system toward straighter wall configurations. Hysteresis measurements reveal that larger field steps produce less remnant magnetic memory, as stronger driving overcomes more pinning barriers and allows the system to approach a more fully equilibrated state.
